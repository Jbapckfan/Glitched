Level 17 — Toggle the device's real Airplane Mode ON to raise flying platforms from their death-plane "landed" positions up to a walkable climb; OFF drops them back into the hazard.
VERDICT: polish (4/5)
STRENGTHS: Genuinely clever fourth-wall mechanic: the flying platforms literally live in the death plane until you flip a hardware toggle, so the puzzle and the theme are one and the same. The per-platform protection (flyingPlatformSupportingBit) plus the reachability-blip debounce shows real care — toggling OFF while standing on a flyer won't yank you into the hazard, and a spurious NWPathMonitor OFF can't kill a stationary player. iPad path is a thoughtfully paced 13-tier climb with named beats (teach/stair/rest/tension/finale) and camera-follow.
CONCERNS: iPad first paint reads as a void: with mode OFF only the 4 solids exist on-screen and the spawn floor (x110) sits bottom-left while the next solid (REST, x705/tier4) is far-right mid-frame, leaving a huge empty middle band and a lone orphan platform outline — the 12 flyers are all hidden in the death plane below, so the screen looks broken/incomplete until the player toggles. On iPhone the panel's right edge crowds under the glowing pause button. The spawn-to-first-flyer horizontal step is 125pt (gap<=130) and last-flyer-to-finale-solid is 115pt — both legal but near the ceiling, so any turbulence X-wobble (±1.5) plus Bit's run momentum makes the upper finale jumps feel unforgiving.
CLUE/HINT: t=0 is well-handled: three atmospheric lines ("CUT THE WORLD LOOSE. LET IT RISE.") evoke flight/tethering without ever naming Airplane Mode, so the trick stays unspoiled. Escalation is solid — notePlayerStruggle() fires on each death and hintText() then spells out "Turn ON Airplane Mode — tap the plane icon or swipe Control Center down," giving a stuck player the explicit how-to only after repeated failure.
SUGGESTION: Show a faint, dashed "ghost" silhouette of each flying platform at its ON/flying target Y while mode is OFF (on both layouts, but critical for iPad). This fills the empty void on first paint, telegraphs that something will rise into that space, and makes the puzzle's intent legible the instant the level loads — turning the current "is this screen broken?" iPad moment into a deliberate "ah, those will fly up" reveal.
