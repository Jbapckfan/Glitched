The header-drag twist has almost no discoverable affordance: the t=0 whisper at lines 109-112 only alludes to “the sign with my number on it,” and nothing in the HUD visually signals that the LEVEL 1 banner is draggable, so first-time players who do not randomly swipe the top edge can soft-lock. The phone pit is 240 design points (lines 40-41), scaling to ~218 pt on a 390 pt screen—well beyond Bit’s ~184 pt running-jump reach—while the iPad variant hardcodes the same 240 pt gap (line 169), making the bridge mandatory on both form factors. The serpentine climb stays within budget because `tierCount` keeps tier rises ≤85 and the clamp at lines 243-245 caps forward gaps at 130 pt, but the drift math is dense enough that a small future edit could silently break those invariants. Hinting is the weakest area: although t=0 text avoids spoiling the trick, `hintText()` at line 819 immediately gives the full solution (“Grab the LEVEL 1 banner and drag it straight down into the spike pit...”), and `notePlayerStruggle()` provides no intermediate escalation between the vague whisper and that complete spoiler. Code rough edges include `drawCables()` at lines 361-396 being fully implemented yet never called by `setupBackground()`, plus force-unwraps on `bit` and `playerController` (lines 5-6, 571-576) that would crash if `BitCharacter.make()` returned nil. The post-accessibility drop logic at lines 662-666 is also dangerously permissive, accepting almost any downward release and therefore blurring the intended lesson about where the actual pit is. Replace the single all-revealing `hintText()` with escalating hints driven by `notePlayerStruggle()` after ~10 s and ~20 s: first point at the banner, then suggest dragging downward, and only as a last resort name the spike pit.
